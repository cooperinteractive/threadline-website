Website Strategy
01 / Storytelling Arc
Pain, Vision, Proof, Action
Every effective product page follows a narrative arc. The visitor arrives with a problem, sees a better future, believes it through evidence, and takes action. PullSheet's website should move visitors through this arc in a single scroll.
Phase 01
Pain
"You're still taping Polaroids to a corkboard?"
Name the problem the visitor already feels. Wardrobe professionals know their current workflow is fragmented: Polaroids pinned to corkboards, fabric swatches scattered across tables, mental lookbooks that never get documented. Make them feel seen. The visitor should think, "Yes, that's exactly my problem."
Phase 02
Vision
"See the look before the fitting"
Paint the better future. Show what the workflow looks like with PullSheet: polished digital lookboards assembled in minutes, garment cutouts arranged on a canvas, everything shareable and exportable. The visitor should feel the gap between their reality and this vision.
Phase 03
Proof
"Built by wardrobe professionals, for wardrobe professionals"
Close the credibility gap. Show features that prove PullSheet can deliver on its vision: AI background removal, the canvas builder, THREADLINE integration, export options. Include workflow speed comparisons. When testimonials are available, add them here.
Phase 04
Action
"Join the waitlist. Free during beta."
Make the action obvious and low-risk. A single email field, a clear value proposition ("free during beta"), and urgency ("limited spots"). Remove every possible friction point. No account creation, no credit card, just an email address.

Key insight: The arc is not four separate pages. It is one continuous scroll on a single page. Each section flows into the next. The visitor should never feel lost or need to navigate. By the time they reach the final CTA, the story has already convinced them.

Section-by-Section Content Strategy
The pullsheetapp.io homepage is a single long-scroll page with 8 distinct sections. Each section has a specific purpose in the storytelling arc.
01
Arc Phase: Pain + Vision
Hero
Purpose: Emotional hook that names the problem and shows the solution in a single screen. The visitor decides within 3 seconds whether to keep scrolling.
Content Elements
  • Headline: "See the look before the fitting"
  • Subheadline: "Build visual lookboards from your wardrobe inventory"

Design Notes

  • iPad mockup (not phone) positioned right-of-center
  • PULLSHEET wordmark in top-left nav

02
Arc Phase: Pain
The Corkboard Problem
Purpose: Deepen the pain. Make the visitor feel the frustration of their current workflow before showing the solution.
Content Elements
  • Headline: "Your lookboard lives in your head"
  • 3 pain points in a horizontal row:
  • "Polaroids fall off the corkboard. Again."
  • "Your director asks for the look. You describe it from memory."
  • "You rebuild the same board for every project."
Design Notes
  • Bone or warm-gray background to shift visual tone
  • Ghosted corkboard image at 0.06 opacity
  • Pain points in cards with clay/muted accents
  • Keep text sharp and empathetic, never condescending
03
Arc Phase: Pain + Vision
Before/After Workflow
Purpose: Side-by-side comparison that makes the value instantly visual. The "before" column is the pain; the "after" column is the vision.

05
Arc Phase: Proof
How It Works
Purpose: Reduce complexity to three simple steps. If the visitor remembers nothing else, they should remember this flow.
Three Steps

Design Notes

  • Each step has an iPad mockup showing the relevant screen
  • Connecting line or arrow between steps
  • Cream background, minimal decoration

Arc Phase: Proof

03 / Conversion
Conversion Principles
Three research-backed principles that should inform every design and copy decision on the PullSheet marketing site.
Principle 01
Urgency
Create a reason to act now, not later. Limited beta access provides natural scarcity without feeling manufactured.
  • "Limited beta spots available"
  • "Free during beta, then [pricing]"
  • Waitlist counter showing sign-up momentum
  • "Early access members get lifetime pricing"
  • Launch date countdown (when set)
Principle 02
Social Proof
People follow people. Even pre-launch, show that others are interested and that the product is real, not vaporware.
  • Waitlist count displayed live
  • Testimonials from beta testers (when available)
  • "Built by a working wardrobe professional"
  • Production logos or studio affiliations
  • THREADLINE connection as implied credibility
Principle 03
Risk Reduction
Remove every reason not to sign up. The less risk the visitor perceives, the more likely they are to take action.
  • "Free during the beta period"
  • "No credit card required"
  • "Cancel anytime, no questions"
  • "Your data stays yours" (privacy assurance)
  • Simple email-only waitlist (no account needed)
04 / Design Execution
Design Execution Rules
The PullSheet marketing site must match the same aesthetic system as THREADLINE. These rules ensure visual consistency across every page and section.

Design Rules

  • Use the THREADLINE design system (same colors, typography, spacing)
  • Light-mode-first approach: cream backgrounds, dark text
  • One dark section per page maximum (THREADLINE connection section)
  • iPad mockups exclusively (PullSheet is tablet-first, not phone)
  • Ghosted photo treatment on hero and CTA sections
  • Lookboard-focused imagery (the output, not the tool)
  • Cormorant Garamond for headlines, Inter for body, JetBrains Mono for labels
  • Brown-gold as the primary action color (buttons, links, accents)

Never Do

  • Use phone mockups (PullSheet is an iPad/tablet app)
  • Show stock photography of models or lifestyle scenes
  • Use bright or saturated colors outside the palette
  • Add animations that delay content visibility
  • Use more than one CTA per viewport (focus, not clutter)
  • Show unfinished UI or wireframes in marketing materials
  • Use technical jargon that non-technical wardrobe pros would not understand
  • Add a dark mode toggle on the marketing site (light mode only)

Imagery hierarchy: The marketing site should show the output of PullSheet (beautiful lookboards), not screenshots of the UI. Visitors should imagine the end result, not the interface. The interface appears in the "How It Works" section and iPad mockups, but it is never the hero image.
