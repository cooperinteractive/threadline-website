Feature: The Canvas

PullSheet Feature
THE CANVAS
Freeform. Not a grid.
LinkedIn Copy
Most digital tools for visual presentations force you into rigid grids. Rows and columns. Fixed layouts. That is not how wardrobe professionals think about looks. When a costume designer builds a lookboard, they think spatially. A jacket overlapping a shirt. Shoes positioned at a specific angle. Accessories grouped by scene. The spatial relationship between garments tells a story that a grid never could. That is why PullSheet uses a freeform canvas instead of a template-based layout. Here is how it works: - Drag any garment from your THREADLINE inventory onto an open canvas - Position, resize, rotate, and layer garments freely - Overlap items to show how they work together as a complete look - Group garments by talent, by scene, or by whatever logic makes sense for your project The canvas has no constraints. No snap-to-grid. No fixed columns. Just an open surface that works the way you already think about wardrobe. We built PullSheet this way because every production is different. A period drama has different presentation needs than a contemporary comedy. A feature film lookbook is structured differently than a commercial deck. Freeform means your tool adapts to your workflow, not the other way around. PullSheet launches Summer 2026. What would your ideal lookbook builder look like?

THREADLINE + PULLSHEET
Two apps. One workflow.
LinkedIn Copy
Why did we build two apps instead of one? It is a question we get asked often. THREADLINE handles wardrobe inventory management. PullSheet handles visual lookbook creation. They share the same data, but they are separate applications. Here is our reasoning: The inventory problem and the presentation problem are fundamentally different workflows. Inventory management is about data: cataloguing garments, tracking quantities, logging check-outs and returns, managing across episodes and scenes. It is operational. Presentation is about visual storytelling. It is about arranging garments in a way that communicates a creative vision. It is artistic. Combining both into a single app would mean compromising one workflow for the other. Either the inventory side gets cluttered with canvas tools it does not need, or the creative side gets bogged down with data management fields. By keeping them separate, each app can be exactly what it needs to be: - THREADLINE stays fast and focused on inventory operations - PullSheet stays clean and focused on visual composition The connection between them is seamless. When you open PullSheet, every garment you have catalogued in THREADLINE is already there. No re-uploading photos. No re-entering data. Drag it onto the canvas and start building. We call it the "scan to present" pipeline. Scan a garment into THREADLINE on set. Present it in a PullSheet lookbook an hour later. Two apps. One workflow. Zero duplicate work. What tools does your wardrobe department currently use for inventory and presentation?

PULLSHEET Insights
The State of Wardrobe Technology in Film Production
An industry perspective
LinkedIn Copy
Every department in film production has seen a technology wave over the past decade. Except one. Scheduling moved from paper to platforms like Movie Magic and StudioBinder. Budgeting went digital with EP Budgeting and Gorilla. Script supervision has apps. Even the accounting department has specialized production payroll software. Wardrobe departments remain largely manual. This is not because wardrobe professionals resist technology. It is because nobody has built technology specifically for them. The tools that exist are general-purpose: spreadsheets, shared photo albums, text message threads. They work, but they were not designed for the specific challenges of managing thousands of garments across multiple talent, episodes, and scenes. Here is the gap as we see it: Inventory tracking: Most wardrobe departments use spreadsheets or paper logs. A garment comes in, someone writes it down. A garment goes to a fitting, someone updates the spreadsheet. When a garment goes missing, someone scrolls through photos in their camera roll. Look presentation: Lookboards are physical objects. Photos printed, cut, and arranged on foam board. This process can take hours for a single talent's wardrobe. When the director requests changes, the board gets rebuilt from scratch. Cross-department communication: Wardrobe communicates with directors, producers, and other departments through a mix of email attachments, text messages, and in-person presentations. There is no single source of truth. We believe wardrobe technology is overdue for the same kind of purpose-built tools that other departments already have. That is why we are building THREADLINE for inventory and PullSheet for presentation. The craft deserves the tools. The tools are coming. What technology gaps have you noticed in your department?

LinkedIn-Specific Notes

  • All captions are 200-400 words. LinkedIn's algorithm rewards longer-form content that generates engagement.
  • Professional tone throughout. Avoid slang, emojis, and overly casual language.
  • Every post includes a call for engagement ("What tools does your department use?" or similar). Questions drive comments, which boost reach.
  • First line of each post is a hook. LinkedIn truncates after roughly 3 lines, so the opening must compel the reader to click "see more."
  • 5-7 hashtags per post. Professional and industry-specific. No trending or generic hashtags.
  • Article cover images at 1200x627 pixels (1.91:1 ratio). This is LinkedIn's recommended post image size for maximum display quality.
  • Post timing: Tuesday through Thursday, 8-10am local time tends to perform best on LinkedIn for B2B content.
